# AI and the Australian Workforce: A Policy Proposal for an Augmentation-First Transition

**To:** Deputy Secretary, Jobs and Skills Australia

**Subject:** Managing the Generative AI transition in the Australian labour market

## Executive Summary

Generative AI (Gen AI) is reshaping Australian work faster than any general-purpose technology since the internet. Jobs and Skills Australia (JSA 2025a) finds AI is currently augmenting more jobs than it replaces, but exposure is uneven: clerical, administrative and junior knowledge-work roles face the largest task-level disruption, while regional and lower-income workers risk being left behind. The Productivity Commission (2025) estimates AI could add over $116 billion to GDP within a decade, yet without active policy these gains will accrue to capital and high-skill workers, widening inequality. This brief recommends an *augmentation-first* package: a national AI Skills Entitlement embedded in the VET system, a Workforce Transition Insurance scheme for displaced workers, and mandatory AI Workplace Disclosure standards. Together they capture productivity gains while protecting equity, costed at approximately $1.4 billion over four years and partly offset by reduced JobSeeker outlays.

## Problem Definition and Context

AI is changing Australian work through four causal channels grounded in the task-based labour-economics framework of Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019).

First, **task displacement**: Gen AI substitutes capital for labour in routine cognitive tasks — drafting, summarising, scheduling, basic coding, first-pass analysis — reducing labour demand within affected occupations.

Second, **task augmentation (the productivity effect)**: where AI complements labour, output per worker rises, expanding scale and indirectly raising labour demand. The *net* employment effect depends on which channel dominates — an empirical question with no general answer.

Third, **task reinstatement**: new tasks emerge (prompt engineering, model auditing, AI governance, human-in-the-loop verification). Reinstatement historically offsets displacement, but only with a lag, and only if the workforce has the skills to perform the new tasks.

Fourth, **skill-biased technical change**: returns rise for digital fluency and "human-complementary" skills (judgement, communication, interpersonal work), while wages compress for routine middle-skill cognitive work — extending the Autor, Levy and Murnane (2003) routinisation hypothesis from manual to cognitive labour.

The Australian context is distinctive. Earlier automation hit blue-collar manufacturing; Gen AI's deepest exposure lies in white-collar services — the sector that delivered nearly 90% of employment growth over the past decade (JSA 2025b).

## Analysis of Impacts

**Employment and tasks.** JSA's *Generative AI Capacity Study* (2025a) finds Gen AI is currently augmenting more than replacing roles, with adoption uneven and often informal — workers using tools independently of formal employer rollouts. The Productivity Commission (2025) emphasises that observable productivity gains will lag adoption, consistent with the Solow-paradox pattern seen with all general-purpose technologies (Brynjolfsson, Rock and Syverson 2021).

**Wages and polarisation.** Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond (2023) find AI compresses productivity gaps within occupations: less-experienced workers benefit most. Acemoglu (2024) cautions, however, that aggregate macroeconomic gains from current Gen AI are likely modest (TFP gains of roughly 0.07% per year over a decade) and distributional effects may dominate. This creates a **pipeline paradox**: if AI does the entry-level work juniors traditionally learnt from, the pipeline of senior expertise weakens. JSA (2025c) explicitly warns that entry-level roles must be preserved as "valuable formative roles".

**Industry composition.** PwC Australia (2025) reports demand for AI-skilled workers in Australia rose from a few thousand postings in 2018 to over 23,000 in 2024, while professional, scientific and technical postings overall fell from approximately 9% to 6.7% of vacancies since 2022. The labour market is *reallocating* faster than it is contracting — but reallocation produces losers as well as winners.

**Distributional and equity lens.** Three groups face concentrated risk:

- **Women**, over-represented in clerical and administrative occupations flagged as highest-exposure (JSA 2025a)
- **Workers aged 45+**, who face higher reskilling costs and shorter payback periods on human-capital investment
- **Regional workers**, where local labour markets offer fewer adjacent roles to transition into when displaced

Without targeted intervention, the productivity dividend will accrue disproportionately to capital and high-skill metropolitan workers — a textbook case of skill-biased technical change widening the wage distribution (Acemoglu and Autor 2011).

## Policy Options and Evaluation

Five options are evaluated against four criteria: **equity** (distributional fairness), **efficiency/productivity** (does it support aggregate gains?), **fiscal cost**, and **feasibility** (political and administrative).

| Option | Description | Equity | Efficiency | Fiscal Cost | Feasibility |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **A. AI Skills Entitlement** | Subsidised micro-credentials in AI literacy via TAFE/VET; 40 hours per worker per 5 years | High | High | Medium ($600m/4yr) | High |
| **B. Workforce Transition Insurance** | Income support + reskilling stipend for workers verified as displaced by AI restructuring | Very High | Medium | Medium-High ($550m/4yr) | Medium |
| **C. Mandatory AI Workplace Disclosure** | Employers >50 staff must disclose material AI deployments and consult workers | Medium | Low-Medium | Low ($50m/4yr) | Medium |
| **D. Universal Basic Income trial** | Pilot UBI in 2 regions to buffer broad disruption | High (short-term) | Low | Very High ($2bn+) | Low |
| **E. Status quo + voluntary guardrails** | Rely on existing frameworks (current National AI Plan position) | Low | Medium | Nil | High |

**Comparative trade-offs.** Option A is strongest on the efficiency–equity frontier: it builds supply-side capability that lifts both productivity and wages of those who reskill, but its incidence skews toward workers already attached to the labour market. Option B closes that gap, providing a safety net for displacement costs Option A cannot reach, but at the cost of moral-hazard risk and weaker productivity returns. Option C is cheap and information-providing rather than redistributive; effectiveness hinges on enforcement capacity (Fair Work Ombudsman) and on whether disclosure changes employer behaviour at the margin. Option D scores high on equity in theory but fails feasibility — fiscally untenable and politically infeasible without bipartisan support. Option E underweights the distributional risks JSA flags explicitly and forgoes the productivity-coordination role government can play in technology diffusion (Productivity Commission 2025).

Crucially, Options A, B and C address **different market failures** and are therefore complementary rather than substitutable: A addresses skills under-investment, B addresses incomplete insurance markets for displacement risk, and C addresses information asymmetries between employers and workers.

## Recommended Package and Implementation Plan

I recommend implementing **Options A, B and C as an integrated package**, sequenced over four years.

| Phase | Timing | Lead Agency | Action | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 2026–27 | DEWR (lead) + JSA | Legislate AI Skills Entitlement; pilot Transition Insurance in finance and retail; release Disclosure consultation paper | ~$200m |
| Phase 2 | 2027–28 | DEWR + Fair Work Commission | National rollout of Skills Entitlement via TAFE; Disclosure standards legislated under existing Fair Work framework | ~$400m |
| Phase 3 | 2028–30 | DEWR + JSA + Treasury | Transition Insurance scaled nationally; first independent evaluation by Productivity Commission | ~$800m |

**Total: ~$1.4 billion over four years**, partly offset by reduced JobSeeker outlays from faster transitions and by AI-driven productivity gains broadening the tax base. The package leverages the Productivity Commission's (2025) recommended approach — operate within existing regulatory frameworks rather than create new economy-wide AI law — minimising compliance costs while still achieving redistributive aims.

**Key Performance Indicators:**

1. ≥60% of working-age Australians complete an accredited AI literacy module by 2030
2. Median time-to-reemployment for AI-displaced workers ≤6 months
3. Reduction in the upskilling participation gap between top and bottom income quintiles
4. AI-attributed contribution to multi-factor productivity tracked annually by ABS
5. ≥90% compliance with Workplace Disclosure obligations by year three

## Risks and Mitigations

**Risk 1 — Deadweight loss in skills funding.** Workers who would have upskilled anyway absorb subsidies, reducing efficiency. *Mitigation:* means-tested co-contribution; prioritise high-exposure occupations identified in JSA's exposure dashboard.

**Risk 2 — Disclosure becomes compliance theatre.** *Mitigation:* outcomes-based design (consistent with Productivity Commission 2025); spot-audits and public reporting by Fair Work Ombudsman.

**Risk 3 — Insurance moral hazard.** Employers reclassify ordinary restructures as "AI displacement" to access subsidised transitions. *Mitigation:* employer co-contribution and independent verification by JSA.

**Risk 4 — Curriculum lag.** Technological change outstrips formal qualifications. *Mitigation:* short-cycle micro-credentials with annual refresh, delivered through industry-led Jobs and Skills Councils.

## Conclusion

Australia faces a generational choice. The augmentation-first package treats AI as a productivity opportunity *and* an equity risk simultaneously — consistent with labour-economics evidence that technology shocks raise aggregate output while redistributing its gains. Doing nothing is not neutral: it is a choice to let market forces alone determine winners and losers. A modest, well-targeted package can capture the productivity dividend while ensuring the transition does not entrench disadvantage.
