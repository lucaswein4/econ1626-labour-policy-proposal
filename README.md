# ECON1626 — Labour Policy Proposal: AI and the Australian Workforce

A 1,200-word policy brief addressed to the Deputy Secretary, Jobs and Skills Australia, on managing the generative AI transition in the Australian labour market.

Submitted as part of **ECON1626 Economics of Labour Markets**, RMIT University.

## How to read the proposal

Open [`proposal.md`](./proposal.md) directly in GitHub — it renders with full Markdown formatting, including the policy options matrix.

The proposal follows the structure required by the assignment brief:

1. **Executive Summary** (~140 words)
2. **Problem Definition and Context** — the four economic channels through which AI changes work
3. **Analysis of Impacts** — employment, wages, industry composition, and a distributional/equity lens
4. **Policy Options** — five options evaluated against equity, productivity, fiscal cost and feasibility
5. **Recommended Package and Implementation Plan** — three-policy package, 4-year rollout, KPIs
6. **Risks and Mitigations**
7. **References**

## Evidence base

The proposal draws on Australian and international evidence published in 2024–2025, including:

- Jobs and Skills Australia, *Our Gen AI Transition* (2025) and *Jobs and Skills Report 2025*
- Productivity Commission, *Harnessing Data and Digital Technology — Final Report* (December 2025)
- Australian Government, *National AI Plan* (December 2025)
- Acemoglu (2024), *The Simple Macroeconomics of AI*
- Brynjolfsson, Li and Raymond (2023), *Generative AI at Work*
- PwC Australia (2025), *AI Jobs Barometer*

Full citations are in the References section of the proposal.
